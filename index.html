Names & Culture
My Name and Its Meaning
7th Grade
English
2/2/2026
~55 min
Objectives
1
Understand that names can have cultural and personal meanings.
2
Talk about their own name in simple English.
3
Ask and answer questions about names.
4
Write a short paragraph about their name.
Lesson Procedure
Name Brainstorm
8–10 min
Teacher writes "What can a name tell us?" Students share ideas — culture, family, meaning. Teacher records on board.
ListeningSpeaking
Name Matching
12 min
Teacher gives list of names + meanings. Students match each. (Amira→princess, Yessmine→flower, Noor→light)
ReadingThinking
Find Someone Who…
15 min
Students walk around finding classmates who: have a meaningful name, were named after family, know their name's origin.
SpeakingInteraction
Personal Writing
15 min
Students write "My Name and Its Story" — who chose it, its meaning, why it matters to them.
Writing
Exit Ticket
5–8 min
"One new thing I learned about names today is…" Teacher collects for formative assessment.
Reflection
Revised Version
My Name and Its Meaning

The revised version maintains the same structure with explicitly separated assessment criteria.

Assessment
1
Observe participation during the brainstorm activity.
2
Check answers in the name matching task.
3
Monitor speaking during "Find Someone Who…"
4
Evaluate written paragraph for clarity and correctness.
5
Review exit tickets to assess understanding.

The Dual World of Dreams
9th Grade
Lower Intermediate
60 min
Goal: Distinguish between "sleep dream" and "life dream"; express personal goals fluently.

Vocab: Vivid, Nightmare, Ambition, Obstacle, Achieve, Symbolize

Grammar: Past Simple vs. Future Intentions (want to / hope to)
Lesson Procedure
1 · Hook
10 min
Create a "Dream" mind-map. Pictionary: student draws "Nightmare" / "Success/Goal" — class guesses.
S→STop-Down Intro
2 · Listening
10 min
Teacher reads "The Dreamer's Story." Students show finger signals: 1 = sleep dream, 2 = life goal.
T→SGlobal Understanding
3 · Vocabulary
10 min
Introduce 6 key words. Match vocabulary slips to definitions (Ambition = A strong desire to achieve).
T→SBottom-Up Support
4 · Reading
10 min
Provide "Architect of Dreams" text. Underline one obstacle and circle one achievement.
SScanning
5 · Speaking
10 min
Class Mingle using "Find Someone Who" grid. "Do you dream of living abroad?"
S→SFluency Practice
6 · Writing
10 min
Dream Capsule: write a 10-line paragraph to your future self about your #1 goal.
SConsolidation
Materials
📋
Whiteboard & Markers — for mind map and Pictionary
📄
Printed Handouts — text + speaking grid + exit ticket
✂️
Small Paper Slips — Pictionary words (Nightmare, Career…)
🎙
Teacher's Script — short listening story
⏱
Timer/Clock — keep Mingle and Pictionary on schedule
Assessment
👂
Monitor correct use of "Do you…?" vs "Did you…?" during mingle.
🗣
Ask 3 students to share their Dream Capsule goal with the class.

The Ship That Disappeared
Reading Text (B1)

In April 1912, a famous ship called the Titanic left England on its first voyage to New York. The ship carried more than 2,000 passengers and people believed it was unsinkable.

However, on a cold night, the Titanic hit a huge iceberg. The ice made a hole in the ship and water started to enter. Slowly, the ship began to sink.

There were not enough lifeboats for everyone. Many people did not survive. After a few hours, the Titanic disappeared under the sea.

For 73 years, the ship stayed at the bottom of the ocean. In 1985, a group of explorers finally found it again.

Adapted from National Geographic Kids – Titanic
Target Vocabulary
Voyage — long sea journey
Unsinkable — impossible to sink
Lifeboat — emergency boat
Wreck — destroyed ship remains
Explorer — searches lost places
Disaster — sudden great loss
Journal
5 reflections · Feb 21 – Mar 28
Saturday, February 21st, 2026
The Six Principles of TESOL
The introductory session focused on the Six Principles of TESOL as the framework for effective language instruction. We learned that the foundation of a good classroom is knowing your learners — their linguistic levels and personal interests. A teacher's role is to foster a low-anxiety environment and design lessons that integrate the four skills while remaining flexible.
💭 Read Reflection▾
This session shifted my perspective on what it means to be a "good" teacher. I previously thought teaching was simply about following a lesson plan perfectly. Now, I realize that my ability to adapt in real-time to my students' needs is far more important. The idea of creating a "safe space" for mistakes really resonated with me — if students are too afraid to speak, they won't learn.
Saturday, February 28th, 2026
L2 Writing Development
This class focused on L2 Writing development and the distinction between input and output skills. A key concept was the "Writing Process Approach" — treating writing as a recursive cycle of prewriting, drafting, revising, and editing rather than a single assignment. We also explored the "Sandwich" concept of layering ideas logically. (Self-study — absent)
💭 Read Reflection▾
Even though I missed the live discussion, reviewing the "process approach" was eye-opening. I used to view writing as a stressful, one-shot task. Seeing it as a cycle of improvement makes it much more approachable for students. I now understand that students need a solid "input" foundation before they can produce high-quality "output."
Saturday, March 7th, 2026
Reading Strategies & Memory
Today's session covered three primary reading models: Bottom-up (decoding words), Top-down (using background knowledge), and the Interactive Model — considered most effective as it combines both. We also discussed using visual aids, repetition, and prior knowledge connections to improve memory and context clue recognition.
💭 Read Reflection▾
I found the interactive model of reading to be the most practical tool for my future classroom. It taught me that I shouldn't just focus on vocabulary lists — I need to help students use what they already know to solve linguistic puzzles. Reading is an active process of constructing meaning, and I want to choose texts that are relevant to my students' lives.
Saturday, March 14th, 2026
Assessment in Language Teaching
This session covered the three assessment types: Diagnostic (pre-tests), Formative (ongoing feedback), and Summative (final grades). We discussed Validity vs. Reliability, and learned that a good assessment should be authentic and fair — reflecting actual language abilities rather than just test-taking skill.
💭 Read Reflection▾
Before this session, I viewed assessment as a "judgment" on the student. Now, I see it as a diagnostic tool for the teacher. Formative assessment seems like the best way to support students because it allows for correction during the learning process. I want my future assessments to feel like a supportive check-in that builds student confidence.
Saturday, March 28th, 2026
Oral Skills & Classroom Management
The final session focused on Academic Oral Skills and Classroom Management — three pillars: Environment (physical setup), Student (praise and clear rules), and Lesson (pacing and engagement). We practiced non-verbal signals like the "Rhythm Clap" and "Give me 5." We also briefly discussed AI tools like ChatGPT for brainstorming while maintaining academic integrity.
💭 Read Reflection▾
The most inspiring part of today was seeing how firm discipline and fun can go hand-in-hand. The management signals we practiced showed me that I can regulate the "energy" of the room without being a "loud" teacher. I feel much more confident knowing that a successful lesson is a balance of authentic communication and clear, consistent structure.
